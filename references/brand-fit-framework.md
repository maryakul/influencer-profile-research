# Brand-fit framework: building brand context, scoring fit, stance, and outreach

## 1. Build the brand context (from the user's company link)

Fetch the company site and write down, in one short block at the top of your working notes:

- **What they sell** — product/service, price point, B2B/B2C.
- **ICP** — who the ideal customer is, as specifically as the site allows (job, seniority, niche, mindset). If the site is vague, infer from features/pricing/testimonials and say you inferred it.
- **Positioning and values** — what the brand is FOR and AGAINST (e.g., anti-hype, craft-first, privacy-first, speed, luxury). The "against" matters most for creator fit: it defines which creators are allies and which adjacency would damage the brand.
- **Tone** — calm/loud, technical/literary, playful/serious. A creator whose tone clashes with the brand's makes even a well-targeted placement feel off.
- **The core contested topic**, if any — the subject the brand's audience argues about (AI for writing tools, sustainability for fashion, privacy for consumer tech). This drives the stance analysis below.

Reuse this block across creators for the same company instead of re-deriving it.

## 2. The four fit axes (score each, then give one overall x/10)

1. **Demographic** — is this audience the brand's ICP? Estimate the relevant share in % (a dead-center niche channel: 50–80%; an adjacent intellectual generalist: 15–25%; a broad business/tech channel: 6–15%). Name the reasoning.
2. **Topical** — does the content overlap the brand's subject matter, and from the right angle? (A finance channel covering "AI stocks" is NOT topical fit for an AI writing tool.)
3. **Psychographic/values** — does the audience share the brand's values (craft, skepticism, depth, speed, status…)? This axis predicts conversion quality and retention better than reach ever does.
4. **Tone/format** — does the creator's register match the brand's, and can the channel do honest integrations (host-read, endorsement)? Personal-brand creators carry endorsement weight far beyond impressions; faceless digests don't.

Calibration: 9–10 = alignment on all four (rare); 7–8 = strong on 2–3; 5–6 = one strong axis; ≤4 = weak everywhere or a values/tone clash. **A 9/10-fit micro-creator usually beats a 6/10 giant** on conversion and retention — but only at a price that matches its real reach.

Separately, always flag **brand-adjacency risks**: political polarization (either direction), doom/alarmism, gray-zone content (e.g., "bypass AI detectors"), the platform running unlabeled ads (FTC exposure), and what previous sponsors sat in this slot.

## 3. Stance scale (toward the brand's core contested topic)

Skip this section only if the brand genuinely has no contested topic. Classify the creator:

| Code | Category | Markers |
|---|---|---|
| 🔴 | Hard opponent | Public, active stance against the brand's category/topic; manifestos, refused money on principle |
| 🟠 | Critic / skeptic | Against the hype and the slop; dissects and mocks, but not a total-refusal activist |
| 🟡 | Thoughtful adopter | Uses/engages with the topic critically — "a tool, not a replacement"; usually the sweet spot for products in the category |
| 🟢 | Enthusiast / practitioner | Builds content or business on the category; check ethics (gray-zone promos = stop) |
| ⚪ | Neutral | Not their topic; establish their stance in correspondence BEFORE showing creative |

Back the classification with **concrete titles of their videos/posts** — that's simultaneously your evidence and your first-email material.

Why it matters — the risk map flips with what you're selling:
- If the user's product **belongs to the contested category** (say, an AI product): 🔴 creators are the public-rejection zone — a "buy our ad" email can end up dissected in their next post; 🟡 is the sweet spot; 🟢 is commercially easy but may dilute the brand.
- If the user's brand **positions against the category's excesses** (anti-hype, craft-first): 🔴/🟠 are ideological allies — pitch the shared thesis directly; 🟢 adjacency may clash.

## 4. First-email rules (by stance category)

The user should meet the creator before negotiating — the first email is almost never "buy our ad":

- **🔴:** no money in the first email. Mission, a quote from their own work, early access / content exchange / interview offer, and a genuine willingness to hear "no".
- **🟠:** lead with the shared thesis; money can appear in the second paragraph.
- **🟡:** shared framing ("a tool for thinking, not a replacement"), business-like tone, fine to go straight to formats and pricing.
- **🟢:** transactional, but with a strict brief (what the integration may and may not claim).
- **⚪:** ask their stance first — before any creative.

Two approach modes:
- **"Kindred spirits" (relationship-first):** personal brands with values alignment — open with a specific compliment on a NAMED piece of their work, propose acquaintance/exchange; money last or not in the first email at all.
- **Transactional:** media outlets with an ad desk or storefront — a standard price/media-kit inquiry; no courtship needed.

Every dossier's outreach section must include: (a) the concrete contact channel (email / form / platform DM / storefront), (b) 2–3 sentences on what the first email says, (c) what to request (media kit, real sponsor-slot CTR, dashboard screenshots — whenever the numbers are self-reported).

## 5. Economics sanity anchors (for the "overall picture" section)

Across a well-run portfolio of niche placements: top-value newsletter placements land around **$1.5–6 per acquired subscriber**; a solid mid-tier $12–26; anything above ~$30 needs a special justification (endorsement value, perfect fit, evergreen tail). YouTube host-reads with a tail can settle at ~$15–35. X and TikTok as performance channels almost always lose to newsletters (suppressed external clicks) — their value is authority and awareness. When the working folder has other profiles or a master table, compare against those real neighbors rather than only these anchors.
